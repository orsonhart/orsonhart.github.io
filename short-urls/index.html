On Short URLs

September 18, 2023

I went back and forth on how to structure URLs for this website. On reading Derek Sivers’ post Short URLs: why and how, I agreed with his reasons - they look nicer and are easier to type and remember - and so thought it would be good to implement his suggestions.

Domain

I decided to use orsonh.art as a shorter (and more fun) domain name, and I was planning on using single word or abbreviated URLs. I instantly ran into my first problem. Ease of communication.

orsonh.art is easier to type, but harder to communicate. As a comment on Sivers’ original post notes

Love the philosophy of short URLS. The only barrier I’ve found is communicating them verbally

I tried to explain my cool new URL to a handful of people, and it always took longer for them to understand than the simpler orsonhart.com. Another upside to using orsonhart.com is that .com is a universally recognised, and more sensible, top-level domain. A friend of mine said “orsonh.art sounds like your an artist named orsonh”.

I did, however, drop the www.. It’s much nicer.

Post Links

After writing a single post, one of the disadvantages of short URLs mentioned by qntm became apparent, “You do actually have to think of something.”. It is hard (impossible?) to write a meaningful link using very few characters. I like the fact that a slightly longer, although still concise, URL gives you information about the link you are clicking on.

Very few people will remember or type a URL. Most will follow a link posted elsewhere, and so I decided to prioritise

  • Clarity over conciseness.
  • Information over aesthetic.
  • Cleanness over clutter.
